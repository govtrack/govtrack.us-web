The official record is...

Confusing

What’s a “Motion to Invoke Cloture on the Motion to Concur in the House Amendment to the Senate Amendment”?

Misleading

Senate.gov says this vote is about the retirement plans of federal law enforcement officers. IT WASN’T. It was a key vote on executive powers related to the Trans-Pacific Partnership.

Static

Official sources can’t analyze their own data to reveal patterns, like who’s missed the most votes, nor in many cases would we want them to. That’s the job of the public and journalists.

On GovTrack...

Legislative process is explained

“This vote was a vote on cloture, meaning to preclude a filibuster. This successful vote paves the way for...”

How we say a bill became a law...

Political context is revealed

“The TPA initially passed the House 219-to-211. As in the Senate, the House vote was predominantly along party lines, with Republicans supporting the President’s trade deal.”

Milestones

Our Firsts

First to offer free email updates for federal legislative tracking (2004).

First to publish comprehensive open data about Congress (2005).

First to use Google Maps to show street-level congressional district outlines (2006).

First to “diff” bill text, to show changes to bills over time (2007)

First to publish voting records from 1789-present (2010).

First to apply Google’s PageRank algorithm to rank politicians (2010).

First to predict if a bill will become a law (2012).

First to offer free federal + 50-state legislative tracking (2012; discontinued).

First to offer a free calendar of upcoming committee hearings (2014).

Investigative reporting

Members of Congress trade in companies while making laws that affect those same firms
The Washington Post. June 2012.

One-hundred-thirty members of Congress or their families have traded stocks collectively worth hundreds of millions of dollars in companies lobbying on bills that came before their committees. . . . The Post analysis is based on a comparison of federal financial disclosure forms from all members of Congress to a wide array of public records, drawing on work by the Center for Responsive Politics and Govtrack.us to convert paper documents to databases.

Parallel sites in other jurisdictions inspired by GovTrack

OpenStates.org for U.S. state legislatures

High-tech Parliamentary Monitoring Organization (PMO) websites in most countries.

New official data

We put pressure on government to improve their own apps and open data policies, resulting in:

  • The Senate publishes votes in XML. (2009)
  • The House publishes upcoming bills and a committees calendar (2012).
  • Congress.gov offers email updates for legislation (2015).
